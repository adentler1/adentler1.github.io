Teaching

Teaching Philosophy and Highlights

I teach economics as a toolkit for understanding institutions, markets, and real data. The goal is not only theoretical clarity, but the ability to move from a model to evidence, from evidence to visualization, and from visualization to a convincing explanation.

Philosophy

My courses are data-driven, visual, and application-oriented. I want students to understand the formal logic of economics, but also to see how that logic survives contact with messy data, institutional detail, and real-world policy questions. Practitioner-style outputs matter: a strong chart, a compact note, a reproducible workflow, or a presentation that makes a technical idea legible to a mixed audience.

Highlight: Search Theory

Search Theory was designed as a graduate-level course that connected matching and frictional models to substantive applications in monetary economics, labor, and housing. Students worked from the canonical logic of search and bargaining toward concrete questions about liquidity, unemployment, vacancy creation, price dispersion, and market design.

The course was especially useful as a bridge between abstract theory and applied intuition. It showed students how a compact model can travel across fields once they understand the structure of frictions, information, and strategic behavior.

Highlight: Data Visualization as Pedagogy

One centerpiece of my teaching is the data visualization project, which I used in economics courses and later showcased through @LiquidMexico. The assignment asks students to find data in the wild, evaluate its quality, understand the economics behind it, build a visual argument, and present the result to classmates. In practice, that means learning research design, teamwork, programming, and communication at the same time.

The best student projects often become the first step toward a thesis because the assignment forces a move from passive consumption of datasets to active economic storytelling. Public examples on @LiquidMexico are my own work rather than student submissions, but they reflect the same standard: clear visuals, economic context, and an audience outside the classroom.

Problem-Solving: Pólya’s Method

Much of how I teach and approach research is shaped by George Pólya’s framework for problem-solving: understand the problem, devise a plan, carry it out, and look back. I use this cycle explicitly in courses, supervision, and my own work. It structures how students decompose an unfamiliar question into pieces they can handle, and how they learn to check whether a solution actually answers what was asked. The method scales from a first proof in an undergraduate course to a computational strategy in a research paper.

Student Outcomes

Former students have gone on to strong doctoral and research trajectories, including paths associated with Penn State, Toulouse, and MIT. More broadly, I aim to leave students with work they can show: a visualization, a note, a reproducible analysis, or a thesis idea that did not exist at the start of the course.

Self-contained econometrics modules pairing theory with working code in MATLAB and Python. Each module includes a written derivation, a minimal example, and code that validates itself against known results.

The Delta Method

Theory + MATLAB + Python

Variance estimation for transformed parameters via Taylor expansion. Covers the linear case (Wald tests, exclusion restrictions) and the nonlinear case (numerical Jacobians, the smooth function model). Includes a collinearity analogy for singular transformations.
